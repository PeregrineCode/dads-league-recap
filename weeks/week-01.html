Matchup Mayhem

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Five weeks into 2026 and we've already got a matchup that'll haunt a manager in his sleep. Professor McGonigle and Ep-Skenes Files played 12 categories of head-to-head baseball and walked away with absolutely nothing to show for it — a 5-5-0 dead tie, the fantasy equivalent of a 19-inning scoreless game where both closers blew their arms out. McGonigle ran an absolute pitching clinic — 43 strikeouts, a 2.63 ERA, a 4.78 K/BB ratio that would make a stat nerd weep — and still couldn't drag a win out of it. Ep-Skenes countered with 18 RBI, 14 runs, and a .234 batting average that exposed McGonigle's lineup for what it was: a .165 corpse at the plate. You dominate the mound and still get nothing. That's not bad luck. That's a lineup problem.

Meanwhile, Babe Ruthless handed Juiced Balls a 8-3-0 beatdown that felt like a mercy rule situation by Thursday. Seven home runs, 21 runs, a .362 OBP — Babe Ruthless went full Murderers Row on a Juiced Balls squad that countered with a 6.89 ERA and apparently zero quality starts. Both staffs were disasters, but Babe Ruthless's lineup was so dominant it didn't matter. You don't need aces when you're scoring like the 2019 Astros.

Eephus put together the most quietly dominant pitching performance of the week in a 7-4-0 victory over Jr Jays — a 1.89 ERA, a 0.87 WHIP, and a 5.33 K/BB ratio that belongs in a museum. Jesse's first-year squad is not messing around. Jr Jays won the OBP battle (.411 to .321, which is legitimately excellent), grabbed saves, and piled up strikeouts — and still got bounced. When you're posting a sub-2 ERA, sometimes that's just how it goes.

All Betts Are Off had themselves a week, taking down Cease and Desist 7-5-0 on the strength of eight home runs, 24 RBI, and a run total (23) that makes the offense look like it swallowed Coors Field whole. Cease and Desist answered with a 3.24 ERA and a 1.00 WHIP — legitimately elite numbers — but couldn't score enough to survive the offensive onslaught. The pitching was there; the bats weren't.

Misio Soup edged Risky Bettiness 7-5-0 in the tightest won-loss result aside from the tie, and it came down to pitching depth. Misio posted a 0.98 WHIP and a 5.80 K/BB ratio while Risky Bettiness ran out there with a 5.73 ERA and a 1.51 WHIP. Risky fought back with nine home runs and five SV+H, but you can't post those pitching numbers and expect to win. Misio took pitching, Risky took the slugging, and the arms won.

Power Rankings

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Week one. No records. No trends. No excuses. Everybody's undefeated until the first pitch, and then the league separates itself from the pretenders faster than a Dylan Cease slider in a 1-2 count. Here's where we stand after opening week — and I'm not hedging on any of it.

CONTENDERS

These three came out swinging like they had something to prove. Bookmark this section.

#1 — Babe Ruthless. An 8-3 thumping in Week 1 is not a fluke — that's a statement. Jordan won this league as a rookie and almost won it last year, and if that opening-week performance is any indication, the rest of the league is already playing for second place.

#2 — Eephus. Seven-four in your first week as a league member? Jesse walked in here like he'd been doing this for nine years. Don't sleep on a first-year manager who doesn't know what he's supposed to be afraid of yet.

#3 — Misio Soup. Dennis goes 7-5 and somehow I'm already worried about him. Classic Misio Soup — strong enough to be dangerous, just uneven enough to keep you up at night. He's got the talent, he just needs to stop tinkering.

SOLID

Not quite elite, but not in trouble either. These two won and should feel good about it — cautiously.

#4 — All Betts Are Off. Tyler goes 7-5 in his debut season and lands in the fourth spot. For a first-year manager, that's a legitimate strong start. The question is whether he can sustain it when the waiver wire gets competitive and the schedule gets ugly.

#5 — Professor McGonigle. Tom finishes 5-5 and ties his matchup — the commissioner squeaks by without a win or a loss and we're all supposed to feel fine about that? I'm not saying it's a disaster. I'm just saying five-five in Week 1 is the fantasy equivalent of a rainout.

MEDIOCRE

Lost their matchups. Need to figure some things out, and they need to do it fast.

#6 — Ep-Skenes Files. Matt drops a 5-5 tie-breaker and lands just outside the top five. The name promises elite pitching — it had better deliver. Going 5-5 in April with a full season of Pete Skenes ahead of you means you've got no room for a cold streak.

#7 — Cease and Desist. Philip loses 5-7 and I've got concerns. Not alarm bells — concerns. Cease and Desist is a name that implies things will be shut down, and right now it's the offense that's been served the restraining order.

#8 — Risky Bettiness. Nate goes 5-7 and the "risky" part of that team name is doing a lot of heavy lifting right now. There's talent in there somewhere. The bets just need to start paying off.

REBUILDING

This is a polite word for what happened to these two in Week 1. I'll be direct.

#9 — Jr Jays. Gordon drops 4-7 and opens the season in the basement of the standings. The Blue Jays have been rebuilding for three years too, and nobody's writing puff pieces about that either. Time to get busy.

#10 — Juiced Balls. Fantasy Guru — a man with that name in his team's DNA — goes 3-8 in the first week of play. Three and eight. I've seen spring training performances more competitive than that. The Guru needs to find the juice, because right now the balls are absolutely flat.

Best Pickup of the Week

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Let me be clear about something: Professor McGonigle didn't just have the best pickup of the week — he had the best pickup AND the second-best pickup of the week. That is not a coincidence. That is a front office operating at a different level than everyone else in this league.

Dominic Canzone gets the crown. Five runs, two bombs, a .333 average, a .412 OBP — the man touched five categories in a single week and looked like he belonged on an All-Star ballot doing it. Canzone has been one of the more frustrating cases in recent Mariners history, a guy with clear tools who just couldn't stay healthy long enough to make anyone believe. Professor McGonigle believed anyway, and got paid. That's the move. That's the exact kind of speculative grab on an upside player that separates the managers who win this league from the ones who are just logging in.

The same manager also scooped Alec Burleson, who went out and quietly posted six hits, five RBI, and a .370 OBP for good measure. Two of the week's top three waiver wire returns, same roster. I don't know what the Professor is doing differently but I'd like to know. Call it a masterclass in roster management.

Royce Lewis over in Misio Soup's dugout deserves a mention — five runs and two home runs when healthy is exactly why the former first overall pick is always worth the gamble. The .200 average keeps it out of the top spot, but Lewis is the kind of lightning-in-a-bottle talent that can win a week by himself when he stays off the IL. File that away.

Hall of Shame

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Welcome, one and all, to the Hall of Shame — the weekly exhibit where we honor the finest in fantasy mismanagement and ask ourselves, collectively, what were they thinking.

We begin with All Betts Are Off, who apparently looked at the waiver wire, saw Slade Cecconi, and thought: yes, this is my guy. This is the arm that turns my week around. Cecconi went out there against a major league lineup and posted a 12.5 ERA across four and a third innings of carnage. Four innings. That is the entirety of the contribution. He could not get to the fifth. The WHIP was 2.08, the quality starts column read zero, and the only number that wasn't embarrassing was the strikeout-to-walk ratio — which, at 1.67, is the statistical equivalent of a participation trophy. Cecconi was once a top-100 prospect. The Diamondbacks traded him away. That tells you something.

Over in the Babe Ruthless dugout, Jordan slid Lucas Erceg into his lineup like a man slipping a note under a door and running away. The results were about what you'd expect from a Royal reliever who apparently did not appear in a single meaningful situation this week. Nothing to report. That's the bit. The Hall of Shame sometimes inducts for what didn't happen, and this is one of those weeks.

And then there's Professor McGonigle — the commissioner himself — who dipped into the Chicago White Sox farm system and surfaced with Chase Meidroth, a utility infielder on the most aggressively rebuilding roster in baseball. The White Sox finished near the bottom of the majors last year, are rebuilding from scratch, and Meidroth is deep in that process. The stats column is blank. Tom, you run this league. You are supposed to set the standard. Instead, you have filed paperwork to acquire a 24-year-old from a team that currently resembles a Triple-A roster with a big league bus pass. The Hall of Shame welcomes all, commissioner. The plaque is in the mail.

Stream of the Week

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Somebody in Eephus's front office is paying attention, and I want everyone to take notes. While half this league was busy watching their starting pitchers get lit up and their waiver queues gather dust, Eephus went out and grabbed Mitch Keller and got exactly what the label promised: six innings of shutdown baseball, zero runs, a 0.500 WHIP, and a quality start in the bank. That is not luck. That is scouting.

Let me be clear about what Keller is. This is the same guy who completely reinvented himself in 2023 — went from Pirates punching bag to legitimate top-of-the-rotation arm, and he hasn't looked back. You don't just stumble onto that. PNC Park keeps the ball in the yard, Keller commands his fastball and keeps hitters off his breaking stuff, and if he's sitting on your waiver wire, you are leaving money on the table. Eephus picked it up. Eephus cashed it.

Risky Bettiness deserves a mention too — snagging Matt Strahm for a two-strikeout, zero-hit inning is clean work from the bullpen side of the ledger. But a quality start is a quality start, and this week's stream crown belongs to Eephus, no debate. I'm calling the manager "The Prospector" this week, because they just panned for gold and found it.

Trade Desk

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Good context. Misio Soup is Dennis's team, and the trade rationale makes sense — he was consolidating speed into power. Now let me write the segment.

One trade hit the wire this week, and I'm not going to sugarcoat it: Professor McGonigle got away with something on Monday.

The Professor picked up Nico Hoerner and Chandler Simpson. In return, Misio Soup walked away with Josh Naylor. Two-for-one deals almost never favor the team giving up the two pieces, and this one doesn't buck that trend. Hoerner is a legitimate everyday fantasy contributor — .270-plus average, gets on base, chips in 20-plus steals, and isn't going to embarrass you in any category. That alone is worth the price of admission. Simpson is a pure stolen base machine off the Rays, and in a 12-category league where SB is its own line item, that kind of speed specialist has real value as a category filler. The Professor didn't just win this trade — he collected a full roster player and a category weapon.

Now, Naylor isn't nothing. The Guardians' first baseman is one of the better power bats in the American League, and if Misio Soup was genuinely drowning in stolen bases and starving for RBI production, the consolidation logic isn't insane. But you do not trade a Hoerner — a guy who helps in five of your six batting categories — to fix a roster imbalance. You add Naylor off the waiver wire when he's available, or you find a worse player to deal. Giving up quality depth to fix a roster construction problem is a self-inflicted wound, and I'll be watching to see if Misio Soup feels that by the time May rolls around.

Professor McGonigle, I'm calling you The Consolidator this week — because you managed to be on the right side of the other guy's consolidation.

Front Office Failures

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Let's start with the man who is apparently running a revolving door out there in Professor McGonigle's front office. You drop Alec Burleson — Alec Burleson, a guy who hits line drives for a living in one of the better hitter's parks in the National League — and he goes out and slaps together a .273/.370 line with a home run and five RBI like he's trying to send you a message. The Cardinals outfield situation is not complicated. Burleson hits. That's what he does. Professor McGonigle looked at that and said "no thank you" and walked away from a perfectly good piece of furniture sitting on the curb.

Ep-Skenes Files, meanwhile, has apparently decided that Bryan Reynolds is no longer worth a roster spot. The same Bryan Reynolds who put up five runs and two home runs the moment he hit the open market. Look, Reynolds has had his ups and downs — we all know that — but you don't just cut a proven Major League bat loose in week one and act like the decision aged well. Two jacks and a .250 OBP after the drop is what we in the business call "an instant haunting." The Ep-Skenes Files front office needs to have a serious conversation about its player evaluation department.

Eephus, bless their heart, is out here running a charity service for the rest of the league. You dropped Mitch Keller — Mitch Keller, who just went out and threw six innings of shutout ball with a 0.500 WHIP like he's auditioning for a postseason rotation. One quality start, zero earned runs, and some manager is still sitting there staring at his pitching staff wondering why his ratios are struggling. That one's going to sting for a while. The Hammer has been doing this for years and there is nothing quite like watching a starter you just waived go lights-out on you the very next week.

And finally, we have the Chandler Simpson situation, which deserves its own segment one day. Babe Ruthless cuts the young speedster loose, and Professor McGonigle — the same Professor McGonigle who couldn't hold onto Alec Burleson four days ago — turns right around and trades for him. Let that sink in. McGonigle's roster management this week has been equal parts catastrophe and opportunism, which is somehow the most impressive combination of bad and good I've seen since the 2019 trade deadline. The professor giveth, the professor taketh away, and apparently the professor also tradeth for what somebody else didn't want.
