Week 1 Recap (2026-03-25 — 2026-03-29)

Saturday, April 4, 2026

Matchup Mayhem

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Babe Ruthless came out swinging in Week 1 like a team that had been waiting all offseason to make a point — 21 runs, seven home runs, .283 average, and an 8-3-0 demolition of Juiced Balls that set the tone for this entire league. The only three categories Juiced Balls could claim were ERA (a marginally better 6.89 compared to Babe Ruthless's own catastrophic 7.00), WHIP, and four saves. When your opponent needs you to post a 7.00 ERA just to steal the ERA category, that's Week 1 working the way it's supposed to.

Over in the matchup I'll be talking about for weeks, Professor McGonigle and Ep-Skenes Files played to a 5-5-0 tie that satisfied nobody and honestly shouldn't have. Professor ran out a 2.63 ERA, 1.24 WHIP, 43 strikeouts, and a 4.78 K/BB ratio — an absolute clinic on the mound — and still couldn't get over .500 for the week because Ep-Skenes went out and batted .234 with 14 runs, 18 RBI, and four stolen bases and swept every batting category in sight. Two completely lopsided rosters fighting to a draw. Neither manager should be happy, and both are probably pretending they are.

The most statistically confusing result of the week belongs to Risky Bettiness, who put up nine home runs, 18 RBI, 23 runs, and a .283 average — numbers that should bury most opponents — and still dropped a 5-7 decision to Misio Soup. The mound work was the culprit: a 5.73 ERA, 1.51 WHIP, 1.54 K/BB, and zero quality starts. Misio's pitching, meanwhile, posted a 0.98 WHIP and a 5.80 K/BB and just walked through the pitching categories. Five saves and holds from Risky couldn't fix a five-category pitching disaster.

All Betts Are Off made quick work of Cease and Desist in a 7-5-0 decision, with eight home runs, 24 RBI, and four quality starts doing the heavy lifting against a Cease roster that posted elite pitching ratios — 3.24 ERA, 1.00 WHIP, 5.13 K/BB — but couldn't generate enough offense to make them matter. And the early-season headline nobody saw coming: Eephus, piloted by first-year manager Jesse, opened the season with a 7-4-0 win over Jr Jays behind a 1.89 ERA, 0.87 WHIP, and a 5.33 K/BB that made the rest of this league's pitching staffs look like they were still on the bus. Jr Jays had a legitimate .411 OBP and 44 strikeouts from the rotation — but when the other team is running those numbers on the mound, there's only so much you can do.

Power Rankings

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Week 1 is in the books. Power rankings are live. No hedging, no caveats, no "ask me again in six weeks" — the data exists and this is what it says.

Contenders

The top tier earned it with performances that don't need context.

1. Babe Ruthless — An 8-3-0 opening week with 21 runs, seven home runs, and a .283 average is a masterclass no matter what week it comes in. The 7.00 ERA is a liability that'll need addressing, but those bats alone could carry this team deep into the season.

2. Eephus — A first-year manager opening with a 1.89 ERA, 0.87 WHIP, and 5.33 K/BB in a 7-4-0 win is not supposed to happen, and every veteran in this league should be uncomfortable right now.

3. Misio Soup — Won 7-5 against a team that hit nine home runs. When your pitching is so dominant that opponent offense becomes irrelevant, that's genuine roster depth, not a fluke.

Solid

Winning, but with something to prove.

4. All Betts Are Off — Eight home runs, 24 RBI, four quality starts — the offense is a freight train and this team's ceiling is real. The 4.62 ERA is the one thing standing between All Betts and a contender tag.

5. Professor McGonigle — A 2.63 ERA, 1.24 WHIP, and 43 strikeouts and the Professor walks away with a tie. The pitching is legitimate. The .165 batting average is not, and no amount of strikeouts fixes that column.

Mediocre

Each of these three teams has a real strength and a critical flaw that already showed up in Week 1.

6. Ep-Skenes Files — The offense is functional: 14 runs, 18 RBI, .234 average, four steals. A 4.58 ERA and just 16 strikeouts in 17.2 innings is the reason they came away with a draw instead of a win.

7. Cease and Desist — A 1.00 WHIP and a 5.13 K/BB puts the pitching staff in elite company this week. A .188 average and 13 runs tells you exactly why they still lost 5-7. The talent is there — it's just all concentrated in one place.

8. Risky Bettiness — Nine home runs. Eighteen RBI. Still lost. A 5.73 ERA and zero quality starts is a five-category gift to the opposition, and there is no slugging percentage that makes that math work.

Rebuilding

Ground to make up, and they know it.

9. Jr Jays — A .411 OBP and five saves and holds from the bullpen suggest real pieces to work with. A 4-7 Week 1 finish says the roster isn't connecting yet, and the run production categories are where this team has to answer the question.

10. Juiced Balls — A 6.89 ERA, two home runs, and 14 runs in the opening week. Four saves provided the margin that kept this from being a complete shutout, but Week 1 filed a losing motion and the appeal isn't looking promising.

Best Pickup of the Week

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Professor McGonigle walks out of the week with the hardware, and it's not even close. Dominic Canzone — a guy who missed most of 2024 with a shoulder injury and spent spring camp fighting for a roster spot — came out swinging like a man with something to prove. Five runs, two home runs, a .333 average, a .412 OBP. That's not a hot week, that's a declaration. Professor McGonigle identified the value before the rest of this league was paying attention, and that's the kind of move that separates champions from also-rans.

The professor nearly doubled up on himself with Alec Burleson — six hits, five RBI, solid average — and Misio Soup quietly added Royce Lewis, who reminded this league exactly what he's capable of when he's actually on the field. Lewis is a franchise-altering talent when healthy, and healthy is the operative word for a guy who's shredded his knee more than once. Two home runs, five runs, and a stolen base in one week is the kind of output that makes you overlook the risk. Almost.

But Canzone is the pick of the week. An undervalued piece on a Mariners offense that's sneaking up on people, and Professor McGonigle had his eyes wide open.

Hall of Shame

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Somewhere in a fantasy war room, All Betts Are Off looked at Slade Cecconi and saw a starting pitcher. The rest of us looked at the line — 4.1 innings, 12.46 ERA, 2.08 WHIP — and saw a crime scene. Cecconi came into this season as a reclamation project on Cleveland's depth chart, the kind of arm you pick up in a pinch and immediately regret. All Betts Are Off found out the hard way. No quality start, barely got out of the fourth inning, and posted a WHIP that would make a Little League coach wince. This goes in the Hall immediately.

Babe Ruthless picking up Lucas Erceg deserves honorable mention, not because of what Erceg did — he appears to have done precisely nothing — but because Babe Ruthless burned a roster move on a Kansas City bullpen arm with nothing to show for it. Erceg had a decent run closing for Oakland, but he's in a new city, a new role, and apparently a new hobby that isn't recording outs.

And then there's Professor McGonigle, riding high on the best pickups of the week, who apparently couldn't help himself and added Chase Meidroth of the Chicago White Sox. I'll say it plainly: you cannot trust a White Sox player to help your fantasy roster in 2026. The numbers, per the complete absence of any numbers here, agree.

Stream of the Week

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Let me be direct: Mitch Keller is a real, legitimate starting pitcher who just gave Eephus exactly what you stream someone for. Six innings, zero earned runs, 0.500 WHIP, a quality start. Keller has been one of the better streaming options in fantasy for two years running — he misses bats, keeps the ball in the yard, and eats innings on a bad Pittsburgh team that ensures he's perpetually available. Eephus streamed him and got the full package.

Matt Strahm tossed a clean inning with two strikeouts for Risky Bettiness — fine, you take it, but a single inning of relief work isn't going to move the needle in a categories league. And whatever Babe Ruthless got from Lucas Erceg appears to have been, again, precisely nothing. Erceg has now appeared twice in this column for being a ghost.

The real story is Keller. When a stream delivers a zero ERA, a quality start, and a sub-one WHIP, you don't need a crystal ball to crown it. The Hammer has spoken.

Trade Desk

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Let me walk you through what happened here. Professor McGonigle sent Josh Naylor to Misio Soup and came back with Nico Hoerner and Chandler Simpson. That's two players for one. One of those players is a legitimate middle infield anchor who runs well, hits for average, and contributes across multiple categories. The other runs like a man being chased by something.

Misio Soup got Naylor — a left-handed slugger with real power and the kind of professional swing that produces RBIs in any lineup. I won't pretend the return is nothing. But Hoerner alone was approaching fair value, and adding Simpson's legs on top of it? In a league that counts stolen bases, speed is currency, and Professor McGonigle just went to the ATM. Misio Soup got a bat. The professor got a committee.

The verdict: Professor McGonigle wins this trade in a walk. Naylor has had his injury concerns, and Misio Soup is banking on one piece. McGonigle got depth, speed, and a top-line 2B. That's not highway robbery — Naylor is a real player — but I'd take that deal every single time.

Front Office Failures

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Where do I even start. Let's go alphabetically by regret. Eephus drops Mitch Keller — apparently unconvinced that a Pirates starter who has quietly become one of the better fantasy streamers in baseball deserved a roster spot — and then, in the same week, picks him back up to stream him. Congratulations, you created your own problem and then solved it. The panic drop is one thing. The panic re-add when nobody else grabbed him is another. Eephus dodged a bullet of their own manufacture, which is the most embarrassing kind of bullet.

Ep-Skenes Files dropped Bryan Reynolds and watched him post five runs and two home runs. Reynolds is a professional hitter who has been above-average his entire career — a patient, switchhitting outfielder who makes contact, works counts, and shows up. Ep-Skenes Files apparently saw none of that and cut him loose in the second week of the season. That's the kind of decision that should be entered into evidence.

But the masterpiece of mismanagement belongs to Professor McGonigle, who dropped Alec Burleson — and I cannot stress this enough — in the same week Burleson hit .273, drove in five runs, and contributed across multiple categories. The professor then turned around and acquired Chandler Simpson via trade. You gave away Burleson for free and paid for a speedster. That's a rounding error in the wrong direction, and the math does not flatter.

The cherry on top? Babe Ruthless had dropped Simpson just days before Professor McGonigle went and traded for him. The hot potato was already sitting on the counter cooling off, and the professor reached out and grabbed it anyway. Someone in this league is going to win a title one day and none of us will be able to explain how they got there.

The Numbers Don't Lie

by Gerald R. Pemberton III

Risky Bettiness hit nine home runs this week — the league's best power output by a wide margin, against an average of 4.8. They batted .283, also a league high. They scored 23 runs. They lost 7-5. The culprit is straightforward: a 5.73 ERA, a K/BB of 1.540 (worst in the league), and zero quality starts. Power production at the top of the leaderboard doesn't save you when your pitching staff is surrendering the categories it touches.

The inverse case is Babe Ruthless, who won 8-3 despite posting the league's worst ERA at 7.00 and a WHIP of 1.670. No quality starts, no saves or holds. They effectively conceded the back half of the pitching ledger and still walked away with the best record this week, because their lineup hit .283, slugged seven home runs, and scored 21 runs. In a balanced 6/6 category split, you can absorb a wipeout in one department if the other runs deep enough. Babe Ruthless is a working proof of concept.

Cease and Desist had the week's most thankless performance. A 3.24 ERA, a 1.000 WHIP, a 5.13 K/BB — solid numbers across the board on the mound. Their pitching went 2-4 in category matchups. The offense batted .188, put up 13 runs, and mustered three home runs, which is what dragged the final line to a 7-5 loss against All Betts Are Off. The data doesn't care that your starters did their job. It counts categories.

One more number worth noting: Jr Jays posted a .411 OBP, the highest in the league, and scored 13 runs. For context, Misio Soup posted a .375 OBP and scored 20. Getting on base is a necessary condition for scoring runs, not a sufficient one.
