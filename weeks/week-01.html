Week 1 Recap (2026-03-25 — 2026-03-31)

Friday, April 3, 2026 — The League Report Staff

Matchup Mayhem

The Ep-Skenes Files and Professor McGonigle played the most absurd game of the week: a 5-5-0 dead heat that fantasy math somehow resolved in Ep-Skenes' favor. McGonigle's pitching staff was genuinely elite — 43 strikeouts in 27.1 innings, a 2.63 ERA, a 1.24 WHIP, and a 4.78 K/BB that would draw comparisons to peak Kershaw — but their offense batted .165 with a .243 OBP, which is less a lineup and more a social experiment. Ep-Skenes countered with 18 RBI, four steals, and wins in five of six batting categories to drag the split in their direction. Professor McGonigle pitched a masterpiece and lost the war anyway. That's fantasy baseball.

Misio Soup edged Risky Bettiness 7-5-0 in the week's second-closest contest, with OBP serving as the tiebreaker: .375 to .358, a margin so thin you need a spreadsheet to feel it. Risky had the power week — nine home runs, 23 runs, 18 RBI — and nothing to show for it. Their pitching gave it all back: a 5.73 ERA, a 1.51 WHIP, and a 1.54 K/BB that amounted to free bases on demand. Misio Soup's staff countered with a 0.98 WHIP and a 5.80 K/BB to sweep the pitching ratios and take the win. Momentum is a pitching staff that doesn't walk everyone.

All Betts Are Off dismantled Cease and Desist 7-5-0 on the back of eight home runs, 24 RBI, and 23 runs that made the final score look almost polite. Cease and Desist had a legitimate pitching week — a 3.24 ERA, a spotless 1.00 WHIP, and a 5.13 K/BB — but when your opponent is hitting like the '27 Yankees, clean ratio stats feel like bringing a metronome to a drum solo. The OBP category was decided by .046, the only real nail-biter in a matchup where the offense settled things early.

Eephus put together a dominant 7-4-0 win over Jr Jays behind a pitching performance that bordered on embarrassing for the opposition: a 1.89 ERA, a 0.87 WHIP, and a 5.33 K/BB that looked like a closing argument for a first-year manager's credibility. Jr Jays had no trouble at the plate — .270/.411 and 44 strikeouts from their pitching staff — but couldn't match the ratio dominance on the mound. Both squads tallied three quality starts, the lone dead heat in the matchup; everything else broke Eephus' way.

The week's blowout award goes to Babe Ruthless, who beat Juiced Balls 8-3-0 in a matchup notable mostly for how catastrophic both pitching staffs looked. Babe Ruthless posted a 7.00 ERA. Juiced Balls answered with 6.89. Neither team had a quality start. It was not a week either rotation will be putting on their highlight reel. But Babe Ruthless' offense — seven home runs, 21 runs, a .283/.362 slash — didn't need the pitchers to show up, and Juiced Balls' four saves were thin cover for an 8-3 loss that Babe Ruthless filed comfortably under "not our problem."

Best Pickup of the Week

Drake Baldwin just put together the kind of catcher week that makes you question every roster decision you've ever made. Seven hits, three home runs, seven RBI, six runs, a .318 average, and a .423 on-base percentage — from a catcher. Let that sink in. While managers across the league were settling for the usual backstop misery of a .210 average and a smattering of singles, Misio Soup was watching their guy go full J.T. Realmuto in his prime. This was not a one-category contribution. This was a multi-category demolition.

The pickup looks even better in context. Baldwin is an Atlanta Brave hitting in a real lineup, and Misio Soup recognized it before the rest of the league could react. That's the move. Not waiting, not watching, not "I'll grab him if he has another good game." Just acting. Credit where it's due — this was a sharp read at a position where sharp reads are rare.

The runners-up deserve a mention. Chase DeLauter went out and slugged four home runs for Babe Ruthless, which is the kind of power outburst that wins you the HR category by itself and makes managers panic-stream relievers to hold WHIP. JJ Wetherholt contributed across the board for Ep-Skenes Files with eligibility at three infield spots, which is its own kind of luxury. Both were legitimate adds. Neither was Drake Baldwin's week, and this week, Drake Baldwin's week was something else entirely.

Hall of Shame

The Hall of Shame exists because early April is when optimism and the waiver wire collide at full speed, and the results are not always pretty. This week, three managers went hunting for pitching help. What they found should be studied in a classroom.

Risky Bettiness wins the week's most prestigious dishonor by picking up Brandon Sproat, who responded with three innings of work, a 21.00 ERA, and a WHIP of 3.33 that suggests he may have been pitching in a different sport. His K/BB ratio was 0.75, which means he was issuing walks at a faster rate than he was getting strikeouts. A strikeout-to-walk rate below 1.00 is the pitching equivalent of a car that gets negative miles per gallon. This was not a dart throw. This was a dart throw in a dark room at a dartboard that wasn't there.

All Betts Are Off took a slightly more defensible swing with Slade Cecconi, who delivered 4.1 innings, a 12.46 ERA, and a WHIP of 2.08. In terms of fantasy damage, Cecconi was marginally less catastrophic than Sproat — roughly in the way that a root canal is marginally less catastrophic than two root canals. Tyler, in your first season, you are learning that a major league rotation spot and a viable streaming start are not the same credential. File this one away.

Finally, Misio Soup — full disclosure, this columnist's own operation — added Jeremiah Estrada and received a 13.50 ERA in return. The facts that are somewhat in Estrada's favor: six strikeouts in 2.2 innings is legitimately nasty, and he did collect a hold. The fact that is not in his favor: a 13.50 ERA. There is no moral to this story that does not apply to the author directly, so we will simply move on and never speak of it again.

Stream of the Week

Randy Vásquez walked off the mound at Petco Park this week with a 0.00 ERA, an 0.83 WHIP, six innings, eight strikeouts, and a quality start. He was a free agent. He was sitting there, available, waiting. And while most of this league scrolled past his name without a second thought, Ep-Skenes Files saw something. That is called scouting. That is called winning.

The line speaks for itself: six innings of near-perfect baseball, a WHIP that would make Sandy Koufax nod in approval, and the kind of ratio numbers — 2.67 K/BB — that quietly wreck your opponent's pitching categories. No fanfare, no pedigree, just a Padres arm quietly going out and dealing on a night when it mattered. Ep-Skenes Files cashed in.

To be fair, this was a big week for that roster's pitching staff. Kyle Harrison also went five with eight strikeouts and a 1.80 ERA — a 8.00 K/BB line that borders on absurd, even if it fell one inning short of a quality start. Professor McGonigle's Jack Leiter chipped in six innings and a quality start of his own with an identical strikeout total. Respectable work. But Vásquez was the cleanest line of the week, and Ep-Skenes Files found him twice over. When you're doubling up on streaming wins in a single week, you're not getting lucky — you're doing the work.

Trade Desk

One trade crossed the wire this week, and it tells you everything you need to know about how differently managers value speed in this format.

Professor McGonigle sent Josh Naylor to Misio Soup in exchange for Nico Hoerner and Chandler Simpson. On paper, getting two players for one sounds like a win. In practice, McGonigle just took on a walking AVG/OBP liability wrapped inside a stolen base lottery ticket. Chandler Simpson is a sensational SB contributor — the man stole 43 bags in limited 2024 time and has the wheels to win that category every single week. But in a 12-cat league that counts both AVG and OBP, Simpson is paying rent in two categories while stealing one back. That's not an asset; that's a roommate who eats your food.

Misio Soup wins this trade, and it isn't close. Naylor hit .274 with 31 home runs and 97 RBI in 2024 — he's a cornerstone power bat who contributes R, HR, RBI, and puts up respectable slash line numbers without tanking your ratios. Misio Soup didn't just acquire a player; he consolidated two roster spots into one clean, multi-category producer. That's the kind of roster discipline that wins in October.

McGonigle's argument rests entirely on Hoerner's health, and therein lies the gamble. When healthy, Hoerner is a legitimate top-12 second baseman — great contact, solid OBP, useful speed. He has also spent enough time on the IL to have his own frequent flyer miles. Professor McGonigle just bet Naylor's production on a player who can't stay on the field and a one-trick pony who'll drag down his batting average every week he starts. Bold strategy. We'll see if the professor gets an A on this exam.
