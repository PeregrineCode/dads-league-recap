Matchup Mayhem

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Five categories apiece. After seven days of baseball, after 12 categories of H2H combat, Professor McGonigle and Ep-Skenes Files looked each other dead in the eye and neither one blinked. A 5-5-0 draw — a stalemate so perfectly balanced it's almost insulting. McGonigle's pitching staff was an absolute clinic: 43 strikeouts, a 2.63 ERA, a 4.78 K/BB ratio that would make a pitching coach weep with pride. Ep-Skenes countered with the bats: 18 RBI, a .234 average, 4 stolen bases, dominating every offensive category in sight. Two opposing strategies, fully executed, completely neutralizing each other. Nobody wins. Nobody loses. The standings laugh at both of them.

All Betts Are Off came into their matchup with Cease and Desist and did not mess around, taking a 7-5-0 victory. The cruel part for Cease: their pitching was gorgeous — a 1.00 WHIP, a 5.13 K/BB ratio, a 3.24 ERA. That's an elite pitching week by any measure. But a .188 average and 3 home runs from the lineup is a slow bleed, and All Betts showed up with 8 home runs and 24 RBI and turned the batting categories into a lopsided sweep. Win the ERA battle, lose the war.

Misio Soup's 7-5-0 win over Risky Bettiness was a classic pitching-beats-power story. Risky came in hot — 9 home runs, 23 runs, a .283 average — numbers that should win a matchup. But Misio's staff posted 58 strikeouts, a 0.980 WHIP, and a 5.80 K/BB ratio, locking up the pitching categories while 7 stolen bases sealed the offensive deal. Meanwhile Risky's ERA ballooned to a disastrous 5.73, surrendering the pitching side almost entirely. Power is beautiful until the other guy's rotation dismantles your ledger.

Eephus put a masterclass on Jr Jays in a 7-4-0 victory, and the stat I can't stop thinking about is that 0.870 WHIP. Zero point eight seven zero. Combined with a 1.89 ERA, the Eephus staff spent the week making hitters look genuinely confused at the plate. Jr Jays had a strong week in their own right — .270 average, .411 OBP, 5 saves-and-holds — but winning four categories against a team that just scorched the entire pitching slate still leaves you looking up at the standings.

Now. Babe Ruthless and Juiced Balls. I want to be precise upfront: both of these teams posted ERAs north of 6.89. That is the foundation for everything that follows. The week was a slugfest between two moundless disasters, and Babe Ruthless simply had the offense to get away with it — 7 home runs, 21 runs, a .283 average, sweeping the batting categories clean for an 8-3-0 blowout. Juiced Balls grabbed saves-and-holds, edged out a marginally less catastrophic ERA, and that was about it. Eight categories to three. A sports tragedy dressed up in bad pitching and borrowed time.

Power Rankings

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Week 1 is in the books, and I don't care that it's only seven days into a 23-week season — the tape doesn't lie. We saw who came to play and who came to collect a participation ribbon. Here are your definitive, irrefutable, not-up-for-debate Power Rankings.

#2 — Eephus. Jesse comes in as a first-year manager and drops a 7-4 win? Somebody should have told the veterans he was supposed to be scared. Eephus is a name to watch, and I'll be watching.

#3 — Misio Soup. A 7-5 victory is solid work, and Dennis knows how to build a roster — this is a man with championship hardware on the shelf. Misio Soup is dangerous and everyone in this league knows it.

SOLID

#5 — Professor McGonigle. Tom went 5-5 in Week 1. The commissioner has seen nine seasons of this league and still ended up in a dead heat. I expect better from a man of his experience and institutional knowledge. Wake up, Professor.

MEDIOCRE

#7 — Cease and Desist. Philip goes 5-7 and slides right into the mediocre tier like he was born there. Nine years of this league and a losing record before April is even over. Philip, I am begging you.

#8 — Risky Bettiness. Nate's 5-7 week is the kind of result that has no interesting story — it's just bad. Not spectacularly bad. Not "lesson learned" bad. Just quietly, disappointingly bad. Nate deserves better analysis than this, but the numbers aren't giving me anything to work with.

REBUILDING

#9 — Jr Jays. Gordon goes 4-7 and lands at the bottom of the standings with a thud. The Jr Jays had all offseason to get ready, and Week 1 handed in a report card that says "needs improvement" in red ink across the top.

#10 — Juiced Balls. Fantasy Guru. Fantasy. Guru. A man whose very team name suggests an unfair advantage. And yet — 3-8. In Week 1. I have no words. I have many words, actually, but I'm choosing to hold them in reserve out of respect for the long season ahead. Don't test me.

Best Pickup of the Week

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Let me be direct: Professor McGonigle didn't just win the week on the wire, he robbed it blind. Dominic Canzone put up a line that reads like a video game cheat code — five runs, two home runs, a stolen base, .333 average, .412 OBP — contributing to five of your six batting categories in one week. That is not a waiver pickup. That is a robbery. The commissioner should be investigating.

Canzone has always had the tools in Seattle. The contact skills were never the question — staying healthy was. When he gets in a groove like this, he hits the ball hard to all fields and does everything you want from a corner outfielder. McGonigle caught him at the right time and didn't let go. That's the move.

The wild part? McGonigle doubled down. Alec Burleson — Cardinals corner bat, legitimate RBI threat against lefties, sneaky-good OBP for a 1B/OF type — went out and posted six hits, five RBI, and a .370 OBP in the same week. Two top-three pickups from one manager is not luck. That is preparation. I've been watching this league for nine years and that waiver haul belongs in the Hall of Fame.

Royce Lewis deserves a mention because when Royce Lewis is healthy, he is a legitimate fantasy weapon — two home runs, five runs, a stolen base. The .200 average will make you nervous, and honestly with Lewis it always will, because the injury history doesn't let you breathe easy. Misio Soup knows what they're getting: a house of fire when lit, but you keep the fire extinguisher close.

Hall of Shame

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Welcome, ladies and gentlemen, to the Hall of Shame — where bad decisions go to die and waiver wire hubris gets its obituary written. This week's inductees did not disappoint.

Let's start with the headliner. All Betts Are Off reached into the free agent bin and pulled out Slade Cecconi, a man who responded to the opportunity with 4.1 innings of pure, uncut catastrophe — a 12.5 ERA, a 2.08 WHIP, zero quality starts, and the kind of outing that gets highlighted in pitching coach firing montages. Cecconi has been shuffled between Arizona and Cleveland like a bag of leftover gear nobody wants to claim, and now we know why. All Betts Are Off saw "SP, CLE" and said yes. The Indians made him available. That should have been the warning label.

Babe Ruthless, last year's champion, picked up Lucas Erceg out of the Kansas City bullpen — a man with no statistics to speak of this week, meaning he contributed absolutely nothing. Zero innings. Goose egg. Defending champs don't panic-add relief pitchers from the Royals bullpen in week one, and yet here we are. The only thing more concerning than a bad pickup is a mystery pickup, and Erceg is currently both.

Finally, Professor McGonigle — the Commissioner himself — grabbed Chase Meidroth from the Chicago White Sox. The White Sox, folks. The franchise that lost 121 games two years ago. Meidroth plays multiple infield positions for an organization that treats winning baseball the way most of us treat flossing: aspirationally but inconsistently. When your pickup is a multi-positional utility man on a White Sox roster, you are not building a contender. You are collecting participation trophies with extra steps.

Stream of the Week

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Let me tell you something about Mitch Keller that most of this league apparently forgot: the man put together a 13-win season in 2023 and has been quietly one of the more dependable arms in a Pittsburgh rotation that nobody watches because, well, it's Pittsburgh. Eephus remembered. Six innings, zero runs, a tidy 0.500 WHIP, and a quality start in the bag. That is not luck. That is reconnaissance.

First-year manager, folks. First year. While the rest of us were staring at our rotations hoping for divine intervention, Eephus went to the wire and found a legitimate major league starting pitcher having a legitimate major league outing. Keller isn't a lottery ticket — he's a guy who knows how to get through a lineup, eat innings, and not blow up your ratios. The 3 strikeouts weren't flashy, but six shutout innings against a real big-league lineup? I've seen "proven veterans" in this league deploy worse arms in must-win weeks.

Risky Bettiness gets an honorable mention for the Strahm grab — two punchouts in a clean inning is a fine piece of work — but you don't win Stream of the Week with one inning. You win it with six. Eephus gets the trophy, the chalk circle, and the right to walk into next week with a little swagger. Don't sleep on the new guy.

Trade Desk

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Let me be direct about what happened here: Professor McGonigle robbed Misio Soup at high noon, and somebody needs to say it. Nico Hoerner is a legitimate big-leaguer — Cubs shortstop-turned-second baseman who hit .274 last year with double-digit steals and an OBP that puts him in the top third of the league at his position. That alone is a fair deal. Then you throw in Chandler Simpson, the Rays' stolen base machine who is essentially a walking SB category, and suddenly Professor McGonigle is walking away with two contributors across three or four categories for the price of one power bat.

Now, I'm not going to pretend Josh Naylor is chopped liver. The man is a legitimate RBI machine in the middle of the Guardians order — the kind of bat that wins you RBI and HR categories week in and week out, especially with a Cleveland lineup that gets on base. If Misio Soup was bleeding power and needed a plug-and-play solution, fine, I understand the logic.

But here's where I get off the bus: this is a 12-category league. Naylor is red meat for two categories, maybe three. Hoerner and Simpson combined? They're touching AVG, OBP, R, SB — and that's before a single HR leaves the yard. You traded category breadth for category depth, and in H2H, breadth wins matchups. Misio Soup handed away the Swiss Army knife and asked for a hammer in return.

Professor McGonigle wins this trade going away. Someone at Misio Soup's front office should be answering some uncomfortable questions this morning.

Front Office Failures

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Three managers walked into the waiver wire this week and came out looking like they'd just traded Babe Ruth for a bag of sunflower seeds. Let's go through the carnage one at a time, because every crime deserves its own indictment.

We start with Professor McGonigle, who cut Alec Burleson loose just in time to watch him go .273/.370 with a homer, five RBI, and three runs. Burleson is a 25-year-old Cardinals bat hitting in a good lineup — this is not a mystery man, Professor. This is a player you hold. The man then turned around and traded for Chandler Simpson, a speed-only flier who Babe Ruthless had already thrown overboard. So to summarize the Professor's week: dropped a productive hitter, acquired a castoff. Masterclass in zigging when every piece of evidence is telling you to zag.

Ep-Skenes Files cut Bryan Reynolds, and Reynolds immediately went out and posted two home runs and five runs scored. Reynolds has been doing this — being good, then looking bad for a stretch, then being good again — for four years in Pittsburgh. He is not a mystery. The Pirates lineup isn't exactly Murderers' Row, but Reynolds in it is still Reynolds. Ep-Skenes had the guy. Had him. And let him walk into some other manager's arms like it was nothing.

Eephus deserves special recognition for the Mitch Keller drop, which is honestly the most baffling of the bunch. Keller went out and threw six innings, zero runs, half-a-walk per inning, and banked the quality start. A 0.00 ERA and a 0.500 WHIP. Those are numbers you put on a poster. Eephus is out here treating a legitimate mid-rotation starter like a streaming plug on a Monday. There's a word for this kind of decision. Several words, actually. None of them are printable.

The Numbers Don't Lie

by Gerald R. Pemberton III

Risky Bettiness led the league in home runs (9) and batting average (.283) this week. They lost 5-7. This is not a hot take. This is arithmetic.

Their pitching staff posted a 5.73 ERA, zero quality starts, and a K/BB ratio of 1.540 — dead last in the league against an average of 3.572. The batting categories broke 5-1 in their favor. The pitching categories broke 6-1 against them. Nine home runs is genuinely impressive; it is also a perfect example of dominating half the scorecard and losing anyway. Misio Soup, meanwhile, won the matchup almost entirely on the mound: 4.50 ERA, 0.980 WHIP, 5.80 K/BB, three quality starts. The team with the best AVG in the league lost to a team batting .240. Worth sitting with.

The All Betts Are Off–Cease and Desist matchup is a cleaner version of the same problem. All Betts Are Off threw 48.2 innings — 45% above league average — and won Quality Starts (4 to 1) and Strikeouts (48 to 41) on volume. Cease and Desist threw 25 innings and posted a 3.24 ERA, 1.00 WHIP, and 5.13 K/BB. Per inning, it was not a contest. But fantasy scoring doesn't pay per inning, and the raw category counts handed All Betts Are Off a 7-5 win. The data point that deserves attention: All Betts Are Off's K/BB was 1.920, second-worst in the league. They were walking batters at nearly twice the rate of Cease and Desist. Innings pitched is leverage — up to the point where the walks start compounding.

Eephus put up a 1.890 ERA and 0.870 WHIP. League averages are 4.47 and 1.27, respectively. Their starters allowed baserunners at roughly 68% of the league rate and gave up earned runs at 42% of the league rate. These are not normal early-season numbers — they're the kind of line that comes attached to a small denominator and a generous strand rate. Worth monitoring, not celebrating. The .194 team batting average suggests the offense is not going to bail out any regression in the rotation, so the margin for error is smaller than a 7-4 record implies.
