Matchup Mayhem

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Week one of Dad's Baseball League 9.0 is in the books, and Babe Ruthless opened the season by committing a 7.00 ERA and somehow winning 8-3. That is not a misprint. Both rotations were basically waving white flags — Juiced Balls answered with a 6.890 ERA of their own — but the Ruthless lineup swung so hard it didn't matter. Seven home runs, 17 RBI, a .283 average. A blowout built entirely on offense while the bullpens argued over which dumpster to occupy. This is how you make a statement in Week 1.

Now, to the matchup that will be litigated on this very platform for weeks: Professor McGonigle and Ep-Skenes Files played twelve categories and neither team won. Five-five, a tie, everyone goes home empty. McGonigle's rotation was a masterpiece — 43 strikeouts, a 2.63 ERA, a K/BB ratio of 4.78 — the kind of pitching line that usually ends conversations. Ep-Skenes Files had a different idea entirely: 18 RBI, a .234 average, 14 runs, while McGonigle's lineup was posting a .165 average like they were auditioning for Single-A ball. The HR category, the potential decider, landed at 2-2. A dead heat. You cannot write a more perfect stalemate if you tried, and I am furious about it.

All Betts Are Off put the league on notice with a 7-5 win over Cease and Desist, which deserves a closer look. Cease had a 3.24 ERA and a 5.13 K/BB ratio — legitimately excellent pitching — and still lost because All Betts came at them with eight home runs, 24 RBI, and 23 runs. You can pitch beautifully and still get buried by a lineup operating at those levels. The pitching categories went heavily to Cease; the counting stats belonged to All Betts entirely.

Eephus made short work of Jr Jays 7-4 behind some of the cleanest pitching of Week 1 — a 1.89 ERA and a 0.87 WHIP is not a typo either, it's just a genuinely dominant rotation. Jesse, a first-year manager, came in and pitched like he's been doing this for years. Jr Jays had 44 strikeouts and an .411 OBP, real contributions, but they couldn't find the run prevention to match. And lastly, Misio Soup picked apart Risky Bettiness 7-5 in a matchup that looked competitive until you read the pitching lines. Risky hung nine home runs and a .283 average, but posted a 5.73 ERA and zero quality starts. Soup answered with a 0.98 WHIP and a 5.80 K/BB ratio. The bats weren't enough to bail out a staff that had no answers.

Power Rankings

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

One week in. No one has a real record. I'm ranking every team anyway.

Contenders

These three teams looked like genuine threats in Week 1, and I'm not walking that back.

1. Babe Ruthless — Won eight categories with a 7.00 ERA, which means the lineup is so good it currently doesn't need a functional rotation. When that pitching comes around — and it will — this team gets truly dangerous.

2. Eephus — Jesse walks into his first season and posts a 1.89 ERA and a 0.87 WHIP. Either he drafted well or he got lucky, and I don't care which one it was because the result is a 7-4 win that looked utterly controlled from start to finish.

3. Misio Soup — Balanced across the board: 0.98 WHIP, 5.80 K/BB, 20 runs, seven steals. That's not one thing going right, that's a roster working as designed. Week 1 versatility is a real signal.

Solid

Good enough to win, not quite ready to be called favorites.

4. All Betts Are Off — Eight home runs and 24 RBI is a mission statement, and I heard it loud and clear. The 4.62 ERA is a problem to solve, but you can work with a lineup that produces at those levels.

5. Professor McGonigle — The pitching was a genuine clinic and the offense hit .165. Something has to give. When those bats finally show up, this team climbs the rankings in a hurry.

Mediocre

These teams have real pieces. They also have real problems.

6. Ep-Skenes Files — The offense is legitimate — 18 RBI and .234 average in Week 1 — but 17.2 innings pitched from the rotation is a pace that can't continue. The pitching needs to hold weight for this team to move up.

7. Cease and Desist — Posted a 3.24 ERA, a 1.00 WHIP, and a 5.13 K/BB ratio, lost 5-7. The pitching is genuinely good. The offense hit .188 with three home runs. That imbalance is going to be a theme unless something changes.

8. Risky Bettiness — Nine home runs and a .283 average with a 5.73 ERA and zero quality starts. The power is real. The rotation is a problem. These two things are currently fighting each other to a draw.

Rebuilding

It's Week 1. There is time. But there is work to do.

9. Jr Jays — An .411 OBP and 44 strikeouts show some genuine production. The 4-7 loss came down to counting stats that weren't there and a rotation that couldn't match Eephus blow for blow.

10. Juiced Balls — A 3-8 blowout loss in the opener is as rough as it sounds. The four saves and holds suggest some bullpen depth exists, but both the lineup and the rotation need to find another gear before this team is competing for wins.

Best Pickup of the Week

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Drake Baldwin is the best waiver pickup of Week 1, and it's not particularly close. Misio Soup went fishing in the catcher pool and came up with a marlin. Baldwin — Atlanta's young backstop who was always regarded as a legitimate two-way catching prospect — delivered 3 home runs, 9 runs scored, and 8 RBI in a single week. That is a masterclass of catcher production. You want to know how rare that kind of output is from behind the dish? Most managers would take that from their first baseman.

The runners-up deserve a mention. Chandler Simpson, also picked up by Misio Soup, posted a .476 average and .522 OBP. That's not a batting average, that's a slot machine paying out. And Babe Ruthless capitalized on Chase DeLauter going full Guardians-prospect-who-actually-did-it with 4 home runs on the week. Hat tip all around. But the crown goes to Misio Soup, who didn't just find one gem on the waiver wire — they found two. Commissioner's office should look into this. Whatever scouting operation Misio Soup is running, I want to shake that man's hand.

Hall of Shame

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Every week, somewhere in this league, a manager makes a move that defies explanation. This week, Eephus provided us with not one but two entries in the Hall of Shame, which is the kind of double-dip excellence we frankly don't see often enough.

First, Eephus scoops up Bryson Stott, the Phillies second baseman who has been quietly regressing for two years. Zero runs. Zero home runs. One RBI. One stolen base, so he's got that going for him. Stott is a player whose name looks great on paper if you printed that paper in 2023 and haven't updated it since. Then, as if that wasn't enough, Eephus also grabs Colson Montgomery — the White Sox's top prospect playing in one of the worst lineups in baseball. Montgomery managed a .182 average and four plate appearances worth remembering. The White Sox are going to cost that kid a fantasy career before he even gets started.

Ep-Skenes Files wades into the Shame as well, nabbing Dansby Swanson for a .176 average and zero home runs. I have respect for Dansby Swanson the man, the World Series champion, the community leader. But Dansby Swanson the 2026 fantasy asset is a different animal entirely, and Ep-Skenes Files is going to want to come to terms with that reality. Two runs and three RBI for a shortstop you rostered on purpose is the kind of thing that keeps managers up at night.

Stream of the Week

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Mitch Keller has spent the better part of four years auditioning for the role of "dependable Pittsburgh Pirates starter," and on the right week, against the right lineup, he can look every bit the part. This was one of those weeks. Eephus deployed Keller to the mound and got six innings of shutout ball with a 0.500 WHIP. That is not a streaming decision — that is a heist. Keller kept hitters off-balance, threw strikes, and put a quality start on the ledger without so much as breaking a sweat. For a rotation anchor in a streaming role, you simply cannot ask for more.

Misio Soup had an interesting week on the pitching side as well. Connelly Early — the Boston lefty still establishing himself in the big league rotation — gave the team five-and-a-third innings, struck out six, and held a K/BB of 3. Not a quality start, but the peripherals were clean. Jeremiah Estrada provided the weekly reminder that relievers are chaos agents: six strikeouts in two-and-two-thirds innings is elite, but a 13.50 ERA and 1.88 WHIP is the kind of line that makes your stomach hurt. He got a save-or-hold in there, so you take the bitter with the sweet. Keller is the clear winner this week — quiet, efficient, and exactly what streaming is supposed to look like.

Front Office Failures

by Chuck "The Hammer" Morrison

Let the record show that Babe Ruthless cut Chandler Simpson. Let the record further show that Chandler Simpson then went out and hit .476 with a .522 on-base percentage for Misio Soup. We are one week into the season, and Babe Ruthless has already handed a division rival what amounts to an outright gift. I've seen charity auctions with more competitive bidding. You don't give away a player posting a .522 OBP. You don't give away anyone posting a .522 OBP. That is an on-base percentage that would make Hall of Famers raise an eyebrow.

Ep-Skenes Files also left money on the table by dropping Andrés Giménez, who responded by batting .364 with a .417 OBP, two stolen bases, a home run, and five RBI. Giménez has battled injuries and inconsistency in recent seasons, which I understand makes a manager nervous. But that is the exact line you're trying to get from your shortstop every week, and Ep-Skenes Files let him walk without a fight.

And then there is Eephus, who dropped Mitch Keller — the same Mitch Keller who this week threw six innings of shutout baseball and earned a quality start for whoever picked him up. Eephus then went ahead and grabbed Bryson Stott and Colson Montgomery instead, two players who combined to do almost nothing. This is the front office equivalent of trading away your ace and replacing him with a jar of mayonnaise. The only thing keeping this from being the worst week in league front office history is that it's Week 1 and there is a lot of season left to get worse.

The Numbers Don't Lie

by Gerald R. Pemberton III

Risky Bettiness led the league in home runs this week — nine, against a league average of 4.8. Their .283 average tied for best in the league. They scored 23 runs, which only one other team matched. They lost.

The pitching ERA was 5.73, and their K/BB was 1.54, worst in the league by a wide margin. Those two numbers explain the whole thing. Misio Soup didn't need to out-slug Risky Bettiness; they just needed a functional pitching staff. Theirs posted a 0.98 WHIP and a 5.80 K/BB — both league-best marks — and turned a batting deficit into a 7-5 win. You can lead the league in home runs and still lose the week. Happens when you give the ball to pitchers who can't find the strike zone.

Professor McGonigle went the other direction. Their .165 average and .243 OBP were the worst marks in the league, and 11 runs scored was the floor. It was the kind of batting line that raises questions. And yet: 5-5. Their 2.63 ERA and 4.78 K/BB were legitimate enough to win four pitching categories outright, and they caught breaks on the two ties (HR and SV+H each finished at 2-2). Pitching can carry an ugly lineup further than it has any right to go.

The efficiency story of the week belongs to Cease and Desist, who posted a 1.00 WHIP and a 5.13 K/BB — some of the cleanest rate stats in the league — and still lost 5-7. The issue was volume: 25 innings against All Betts Are Off's 48.2. Cease and Desist won ERA, WHIP, K/BB, and SV+H. They lost strikeouts by seven and quality starts 4-1. Rate stats don't accumulate. Twenty-five innings in a full week of baseball isn't a rotation; it's a long weekend.
