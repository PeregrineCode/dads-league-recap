Weekly Recap

Week 1 Recap (2026-03-25 — 2026-03-31)

Friday, April 3, 2026 — The League Report Staff

Matchup Mayhem

The most dramatic matchup of Week 1 barely fits on a scorecard. Ep-Skenes Files and Professor McGonigle split twelve categories right down the middle — 5-5, with home runs landing in a flat tie at two apiece. McGonigle's pitching was legitimately elite: 43 strikeouts, a 2.63 ERA, a 4.78 K/BB ratio, two quality starts. On any normal week, that line wins matchups. But the McGonigle lineup went completely catatonic, posting a .165 average and .243 OBP — pitcher-hitting territory. Ep-Skenes capitalized on the corpse, winning five batting categories on the back of 14 runs, 18 RBI, and a .301 OBP. Commissioner, your league has its first split-decision robbery of the season.

The week's least subtle result: Babe Ruthless stomped Juiced Balls 8-3 despite both pitching staffs staging a mutual meltdown. Babe Ruthless's ERA was 7.00. Juiced Balls clocked in at 6.89. Neither team got a quality start. What saved Ruthless was the batting — 21 runs, 7 home runs, 17 RBI, a .283 average, and they even sneaked the strikeout category 36 to 35, which is the fantasy equivalent of winning a foot race in a full cast. Juiced Balls took ERA, WHIP, and saves-plus-holds and called it a season. Week one and the mound is already on fire in South Beach.

All Betts Are Off beat Cease and Desist 7-5, and the Cease pitching staff deserved a better fate than this. A 1.000 WHIP. A 3.24 ERA. A 5.13 K/BB. That's not a fantasy pitching line, that's a postseason audition. The problem was the lineup: 3 home runs, a .188 average, 13 runs scored. All Betts brought the lumber — 8 home runs, 24 RBI, 23 runs — and there was simply nothing Cease could do about it. A 7-5 loss when you pitch that well should come with some kind of condolences card from the league office.

Misio Soup held off Risky Bettiness 7-5 in the week's most interesting tactical mismatch: Risky actually won the batting side, putting up 9 home runs, 23 runs, and a .283 average to Misio's 5 home runs, 20 runs, and .240 average. The difference was the mound and the legs. Misio's staff racked up 58 strikeouts, held a 0.980 WHIP, and posted a 5.80 K/BB ratio that would have been the talk of any other week. Add seven stolen bases and the narrowest OBP victory of the week — .375 to .358, a margin of 17 points — and Misio had just enough to survive a team that outsluggedthem on paper.

Eephus handled Jr Jays 7-4 in a matchup that turned on one of the cleanest pitching performances of the opening week. A 1.89 ERA, a 0.870 WHIP, and a 5.33 K/BB from Eephus left Jr Jays with nothing to grip. Jr Jays actually outhit Eephus in average (.270 to .194) and OBP (.411 to .321), and dominated the saves-and-holds column 5 to 2. Both teams matched at three quality starts apiece. None of it was enough when the run prevention numbers were that lopsided — Eephus's first-year squad is making a statement early, and the pitching staff is the reason why.

Best Pickup of the Week

Misio Soup found a catcher. Not just a catcher — a catcher who hit .318 with a .423 on-base percentage, drove in seven runs, scored six more, and went deep three times in a single week. Drake Baldwin, late of the fantasy waiver wire, produced like he was auditioning for a Joe Mauer biopic. At a position where managers celebrate a .240 average like it's a pennant, Baldwin delivered a middle-of-the-order performance from behind the plate. This is the kind of pickup that shifts a roster's floor for months.

What makes this a masterclass isn't just the counting stats — it's the OBP. A .423 on-base mark means Baldwin was getting on base nearly half the time he came to the plate, and that's not a fluke line, that's a guy who understands the strike zone. Misio Soup swooped at exactly the right moment, and this column will not forget it.

In the runner-up conversation, Babe Ruthless's grab of Chase DeLauter deserves a mention — four home runs from an outfielder is the kind of week that wins category matchups by itself. JJ Wetherholt, picked up by Ep-Skenes Files, gave you a smooth .280 average with run production across five categories and some positional flexibility to boot. Solid adds. But nobody touched what Baldwin did. The bar was set early.

Hall of Shame

Welcome to the Hall of Shame, where hope goes to die at a 21.00 ERA. The pitching wire was apparently a landfill this week, and three managers dove in headfirst.

Let's start with Risky Bettiness and Brandon Sproat, who turned in one of the more catastrophic three-inning appearances in recent league memory. A 21.00 ERA. A 3.330 WHIP. A K/BB ratio of 0.750, meaning he walked more batters than he struck out. That's not a pitching line — that's a hostage situation. Nate added Sproat hoping to stream a cheap spot start and instead got a category-wrecker gift-wrapped for his opponent. The Brewers rotation has been generous to this league before, but Sproat showed up and reminded everyone why he's been lurking on the waiver wire all season.

All Betts Are Off went a different route, grabbing Slade Cecconi for what turned out to be 4.1 innings of 12.46 ERA baseball. Five strikeouts sounds fine until you realize he was hemorrhaging runs at a rate that would embarrass a Triple-A arm on a bad rehab stint. Tyler's roster needed pitching help, which is understandable — desperation makes fools of us all — but Cecconi delivered the kind of line that makes you appreciate a zero-stat week. At least a zero-stat week doesn't actively hurt you.

The most baffling entry belongs to Misio Soup. Jeremiah Estrada struck out six batters in 2.2 innings — genuinely elite — and still posted a 13.50 ERA. That's the kind of performance that defies easy explanation. He blew up, cleaned up some mess, blew up again, and somehow recorded a SV+H for the trouble. Dennis picked him up expecting saves and got a chaos agent instead. Six punchouts, one hold, and an ERA that belongs on a radar gun. The Hall of Shame is not always for the bad, sometimes it's just for the weird, and this week Estrada was both.

Stream of the Week

Ep-Skenes Files ran a two-pitcher streaming operation this week and came away looking like a pitching coach. But the crown jewel was Randy Vásquez, who delivered a performance so clean it felt like a personal affront to every hitter he faced. Six innings, eight strikeouts, zero runs, and a 0.83 WHIP — that is not a streaming pickup, that is a stolen ace.

The K/BB of 2.67 is the one blemish on an otherwise immaculate outing, but when you're posting a goose egg in the ERA column and punching out eight, nobody is asking questions. A quality start on top of it all means Vásquez touched four of the six pitching categories in a single appearance. That is maximum streaming efficiency. That is the whole point.

Kyle Harrison chipped in a second stream for Ep-Skenes Files — five innings, eight strikeouts of his own, 1.80 ERA, and a pristine 8.0 K/BB ratio. No quality start, which stings, but the strikeout-to-walk dominance was real. Meanwhile, Professor McGonigle grabbed Jack Leiter for a serviceable six-inning, eight-punchout outing of his own — clean WHIP, a quality start, solid. Just not quite the heist Ep-Skenes Files pulled off with Vásquez.

Some managers spend all week chasing waiver wire scraps and come up empty. Ep-Skenes Files went twice and hit both times. Call it luck, call it homework — the box score doesn't care either way.

Trade Desk

The Trade Desk opened for business this week with a single transaction, and it's one worth unpacking: Professor McGonigle sent Josh Naylor to Misio Soup in exchange for Nico Hoerner and Chandler Simpson. On the surface it reads as a two-for-one, which usually signals the team collecting two players got fleeced. Not here.

Naylor is a legitimate bat — reliable RBI producer, decent average, hits for some pop. Misio Soup consolidates a roster spot and gets a guy who shows up in the counting stats every week. That's real value. But Professor McGonigle walked away with arguably the better end of this deal by acquiring two players whose combined contribution covers more categorical ground than Naylor ever could alone. Hoerner, when healthy, is an OBP machine who runs, hits for contact, and scores. Simpson is basically a stolen base factory that wears a jersey. Together they're a category-locking engine in SB, and they'll be doing real damage in AVG and OBP on top of it.

Here's the cold take Misio Soup doesn't want to hear: in a 12-category H2H league, every category is equal. Locking up steals every single week is worth the same as locking up home runs. McGonigle didn't get richer in power, but he didn't need to — he bought himself a category stranglehold that will win close matchups all season. The winner here is Professor McGonigle, and it isn't particularly close. Misio Soup got a good player. McGonigle got a good strategy.

Power Rankings

It's Week 1, so there are no prior rankings to reference, no reputations built, no grudges earned. That changes now.

CONTENDERS

Opening week and the contenders separated themselves fast. These three came out swinging and didn't apologize for it.

1. Babe Ruthless — The most dominant performance of Week 1, an 8-3 dismantling that announced this team's intentions louder than a walk-off grand slam. Jordan won this league in year one and nearly did it again in year two; consider this your reminder that the defending almost-champion has unfinished business.

2. Eephus — A 7-4 win is a statement in Week 1, when managers are still figuring out their rotations and hoping their closers don't implode. Jesse is a first-year manager who apparently did not get the memo that rookies are supposed to struggle.

3. Misio Soup — A 7-5 victory, which is fine, workmanlike, the kind of win a veteran team manufactures when the lineup isn't fully clicking. The ceiling here is top-two by May, and Dennis has enough category depth to be dangerous every single week.

SOLID

Good enough to win, not dominant enough to scare anyone. These teams are squarely in the conversation but need to prove more.

4. All Betts Are Off — Tyler came in as a first-year manager and immediately went 7-5, which earns respect but also raises questions: was that a balanced roster or a hot week? We'll find out. The real test is whether this holds when the schedule gets mean.

5. Professor McGonigle — Tom tied at 5-5 and finished on the wrong side of it. The commissioner starting the season with a loss has a certain poetic justice to it, and there's enough talent on this roster to climb, but ties that go the wrong way have a way of haunting standings in tight races.

MEDIOCRE

Not catastrophic. Not encouraging. Just sort of... there.

6. Ep-Skenes Files — Matt also went 5-5 and also lost the tiebreaker, which means two teams this week essentially played .500 ball and have nothing to show for it. The name promises Skenes-level dominance on the mound. The 5-5 result suggests the ace hasn't taken over yet.

7. Cease and Desist — A 5-7 loss in Week 1 is a bad look, but Philip is a league veteran who has been here before. The categories that went missing this week are either fixable on waivers or they aren't, and that answer will define this team's entire season.

8. Risky Bettiness — Another 5-7. Nate's team name has always had a gambler's confidence to it, and right now the house is winning. The pitching categories need to stop bleeding before this thing slides further down the board.

REBUILDING

First week of the season and already in crisis mode. It happens.

9. Jr Jays — Gordon went 4-7, which in a 12-category format means this roster has structural problems, not just bad luck. You don't lose seven categories in Week 1 by accident. The rebuild is real.

10. Juiced Balls — Three categories won. Eight lost. That is not a slump, that is a team-wide malfunction, and Fantasy Guru knows it. The name implies a team built on offense and power; a 3-8 result implies the juice wasn't there. There is plenty of season left to course-correct, but the hole just got started.
