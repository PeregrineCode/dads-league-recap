Week 1 Recap (2026-03-25 — 2026-03-31)

Friday, April 3, 2026 — The League Report Staff

Matchup Mayhem

Five categories apiece. That's not a fantasy baseball matchup, that's a hostage negotiation. Ep-Skenes Files and Professor McGonigle fought to an absolute stalemate this week, with Ep-Skenes Files taking the win in a 5-5-0 photo finish that came down to a literal coin flip between their pitching dominance and McGonigle's offensive production. Ep-Skenes' staff was surgical — a 2.63 ERA, 1.24 WHIP, 4.78 K/BB, and 43 strikeouts — while McGonigle's lineup responded with 18 RBI, 14 runs, and four stolen bases. Two teams, two completely different rosters, one shared destiny. Someone had to lose and that someone was McGonigle, who will spend the week wondering what could have been if his starters had any idea where the strike zone was.

Elsewhere, All Betts Are Off pulled off a genuine heist against Cease and Desist, winning 7-5 despite getting absolutely torched in the counting stats — Cease and Desist outscored them in runs (23 to 13), homers (8 to 3), RBI (24 to 13), and racked up four quality starts. Didn't matter. All Betts weaponized a 1.00 WHIP and a 5.13 K/BB ratio like a precision instrument and walked out with the W. Misio Soup handled Risky Bettiness 7-5 in a pitching-vs-power slugfest, with Misio's 58 strikeouts and 0.98 WHIP doing just enough to offset Risky's nine home runs and five saves. Eephus took down Jr Jays 7-4 in the most confusing matchup of the week — Jr Jays posted a 1.89 ERA and 0.87 WHIP and still lost, because Eephus had a .411 OBP that bordered on cheating and five saves to boot. And finally, Babe Ruthless dispatched Juiced Balls 8-3, a blowout so ugly that both teams posted ERAs above 6.50 — Babe Ruthless just happened to be fractionally less catastrophic at 6.89 versus Juiced Balls' 7.00. Somewhere, a pitching coach is crying.

Best Pickup of the Week

Drake Baldwin had no business going off like this. The Atlanta catcher dropped a 7-hit, 3-homer, 7-RBI week on the league while posting a .423 OBP — at the catcher position, where managers are usually thrilled to get a .240 average and a pulse. Misio Soup saw something, acted on it, and got rewarded with the kind of line that wins four or five categories in a single week. That is not a pickup, that is a heist.

What makes this truly elite is the position scarcity angle. Any OF can pop off for a week like this. Catchers are not supposed to. When your C slot is producing like a middle-of-the-order corner infielder — with an OBP pushing .425 — you have found yourself something real. Misio Soup did not stumble into this; they read the tea leaves early in the season and pounced while the rest of the league was sleeping.

The runners-up deserve their flowers too. Chase DeLauter went yard four times for Babe Ruthless, which would have been the pickup of the week in any other universe — but a .304 OBP in a categories league bleeds points, and Baldwin's .423 just eats that argument alive. JJ Wetherholt put together a respectable all-around week for Ep-Skenes Files, flashing the kind of multi-position versatility that wins weeks in May and June, but one homer and one steal does not close the gap on a catcher going nuclear.

Baldwin is the runaway winner. Add him if he is somehow still on your wire. He will not be for long.

Hall of Shame

Welcome to the Hall of Shame, where we honor the brave souls who watched the waiver wire and thought, "yes, that's my guy."

Let's start with Risky Bettiness and the Brandon Sproat experience. Three innings. Three strikeouts. A 21.00 ERA and a 3.330 WHIP that reads less like a pitching line and more like a ransom note. Do you know how hard it is to post a 21.00 ERA? You have to work at that. You have to really commit to the bit. Sproat walked batters, gave up runs, and left before he could do any more damage — and somehow Risky Bettiness sat there and let it happen to their pitching categories. If you handed those numbers in at a job interview and said "starting pitcher," they'd escort you out of the building.

All Betts Are Off at least got a full extra inning of suffering out of their Slade Cecconi gamble — four and a third innings of 12.46 ERA baseball, which is truly the worst kind of upgrade over three. Cecconi posted a 2.080 WHIP, meaning roughly two baserunners per inning strolled across like they owned the place. The 5 K and a 1.67 K/BB ratio is the cruelest part: enough promise to make you think maybe, just maybe — and then the runs started piling up like a bad debt. Zero quality starts. Zero holds. Just vibes and damage.

And then there's Misio Soup, who picked up Jeremiah Estrada and got the most deceptive line of the week. Six strikeouts in 2.2 innings! A 2.0 K/BB ratio! Even a hold! On paper, Estrada looked like a sneaky dart. In reality, he posted a 13.50 ERA, which means the batters he did retire were just interruptions in an otherwise very productive night for the opposition. Misio Soup got the fantasy equivalent of a restaurant that has incredible breadsticks and a catastrophic main course — you walked out still hungry and somehow worse off than before.

Stream of the Week

Randy Vásquez walked so your fantasy team could run. Six innings, zero runs, eight strikeouts, a WHIP of 0.83 — that is not a streaming pickup, that is a heist. Ep-Skenes Files found this man sitting on the wire, presumably untouched by nine other managers who looked right past him, and turned in one of the cleaner pitching lines of the week. A quality start, a K/BB ratio of 2.67, and an ERA of exactly 0.00. Zero. Goose egg. Put it on the scoreboard.

The truly absurd part? Ep-Skenes Files already had Kyle Harrison in their back pocket the same week — 8 Ks, 1.80 ERA, another wire special. That is not luck. That is a front office operating at a level this league has rarely seen. While the rest of the division was arguing over whether to start their fourth outfielder, Ep-Skenes Files was quietly stacking pitching categories like a man who has done this before.

Full credit to Professor McGonigle as well, who got a quality start and 8 Ks out of Jack Leiter — respectable work, genuinely. But respectable does not win Stream of the Week. Vásquez threw a shutout gem out of nowhere, and the manager who recognized that was Ep-Skenes Files. That is the difference between a team that wins categories and a team that competes for a championship.

Trade Desk

Two players for one. Before we even get into the names, that math already has Professor McGonigle winning this trade on a technicality — and then the names make it worse for Misio Soup.

Nico Hoerner is a legitimate fantasy asset. We're talking about a guy who hits .280, doesn't strike out, steals bases, and scores runs. He's the kind of player who quietly wins you batting average and stolen base categories while everyone's distracted by the home run guys. And then you throw in Chandler Simpson — a man who exists in fantasy baseball for one reason and one reason only, and that reason is stolen bases. Simpson is going to swipe bags all season long. The Rays have him as their table-setter and he has elite speed. Together, McGonigle is getting contributions in AVG, R, and SB from two roster spots, with legitimate upside in the categories that win close matchups.

Misio Soup gets Josh Naylor. A perfectly fine first baseman. Solid power, decent average, will give you some RBI. But Naylor is not a league-winner. He's a "fine" player in the most damning possible sense of that word. He fills a roster spot. He shows up. He contributes. He is not worth Nico Hoerner plus a stolen base machine. Misio Soup apparently looked at a two-for-one offer involving a borderline top-12 second baseman and a speed demon and said "yes, please give me Josh Naylor." Bold strategy.

Professor McGonigle wins this trade going away, and it isn't particularly close. The only way this ages well for Misio Soup is if Naylor suddenly turns into a 35-homer guy — and Naylor has never been a 35-homer guy.

Power Rankings

CONTENDERS

Welcome to the penthouse. These three dropped jaws in Week 1 and look like genuine threats to run this thing wire-to-wire. The season is one week old and the gap is already real.

1. Babe Ruthless -- An 8-3 hammering to open the season is not a statement, it's a manifesto. Nobody else in this league came close to that category haul, and until someone proves otherwise, this is the team everyone else is chasing.

2. Eephus -- A 7-4 win is exactly what you want to see from a contender: no drama, no sweat, just quiet, suffocating category domination. The most dangerous team in the league is the one that makes it look boring.

3. Misio Soup -- Also 7-5, also in the top three, but there's a slight case of "we should have had that one" in that margin -- a couple of those five losses feel winnable. Still a strong week, still a contender, but the ceiling depends on cleaning up the razor-thin categories.

SOLID

Not blowing anyone away, not embarrassing themselves. These teams are in the race but need to show more before we hand them a contender badge.

4. All Betts Are Off -- Same 7-5 as Misio Soup but sitting a rung lower, which tells you everything about how this league feels about their roster construction. The wins are real, the ceiling is the question.

5. Professor McGonigle -- A 5-5 draw in Week 1 is the fantasy equivalent of a tie in the preseason -- technically fine, completely unsatisfying. You want to see a team ranked fifth win, not shrug.

MEDIOCRE

Ah yes. The middle of the road, where hope goes to slowly die sometime around late June.

6. Ep-Skenes Files -- Another 5-5 and another loss on the tiebreaker. Look, the name promises electric stuff and you're giving us batting practice. Paul Skenes did not come to this league to finish 5-5.

7. Cease and Desist -- The 5-7 line is ugly but the bigger problem is how it happened -- this wasn't a close loss, it was a slow leak. Five categories is the floor of competitive; right now they're scraping it.

8. Risky Bettiness -- Also 5-7, also in the mediocre tier, which begs the uncomfortable question: what exactly is the risk here, because right now the only thing getting bet on is a middling finish. Needs a bounce-back week in a bad way.

REBUILDING

Look, somebody has to be here. These teams are not yet in crisis, but they are absolutely on notice.

9. Jr Jays -- A 4-7 loss in the opening week is a rough way to kick off a season. The Jay Jays name implies a youth movement; the lineup needs to start moving. Four category wins against a full week's slate is a tough sell to any fanbase.

10. Juiced Balls -- Three. Categories. The bottom of these power rankings is earned, not assigned, and a 3-8 opening week is about as bad a first impression as this league has seen. There's nowhere to go but up -- which would be genuinely great news if the roster suggested that was coming.
